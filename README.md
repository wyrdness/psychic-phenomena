## Overview

Psychic phenomena, collectively designated psi (ψ) in parapsychology, are alleged human capacities to acquire information or influence matter without using known sensory or physical channels. The principal reported modalities are extrasensory perception (ESP) — including telepathy, clairvoyance, and precognition — and psychokinesis (PK), the mental influence of physical systems. The field of psychical research was institutionalised with the founding of the Society for Psychical Research (London, 1882) and the American Society for Psychical Research (Boston, 1885). Joseph Banks Rhine's Duke University laboratory (1930s onwards) introduced forced-choice card protocols and popularised the term 'parapsychology'. From the 1970s, Charles Honorton's ganzfeld experiments and Russell Targ and Harold Puthoff's Stanford Research Institute remote-viewing program were central; the U.S. government's Stargate Project ran from 1972 to 1995. Mainstream scientific consensus, formalised by reviews including Hyman and Honorton's joint communiqué (1986), the AIR review of Stargate (Mumford et al. 1995), and the Wiseman / French / Ritchie failed replications of Bem (2012), is that psi has no replicable, accepted experimental basis and is best classed as pseudoscience. The Parapsychological Association is an affiliate of the American Association for the Advancement of Science, but the field rarely publishes in mainstream scientific journals.

## Theories & Explanations

### Paranormal / Cryptozoological

**Genuine psi** _(J. B. Rhine, Charles Honorton, Russell Targ, Harold Puthoff, Robert Jahn, Dean Radin, Daryl Bem)_

Some humans, at least sometimes, possess weak but genuine psi abilities — telepathy, clairvoyance, precognition, and PK — beyond known sensory and physical channels.

**Quantum-information / non-local mind hypotheses** _(Some parapsychology theorists)_

Speculative interpretations invoke quantum non-locality, retrocausality, or panpsychist consciousness frameworks to argue psi could be physically possible.

### Skeptical

**Pseudoscience classification** _(Ray Hyman, James Alcock, Susan Blackmore, Stuart Ritchie, Richard Wiseman)_

Psi research, after more than a century of investigation, has produced no replicable, accepted evidence. The mainstream consensus classes parapsychology as pseudoscience.

**Methodological and statistical artefacts** _(Ray Hyman, Susan Blackmore, Open Science Collaboration)_

Reported psi effects are produced by sensory leakage, randomisation flaws, optional stopping, data tampering, publication bias, and questionable research practices.

**Cognitive biases and folklore transmission** _(Susan Blackmore, Chris French, Daniel Kahneman (background work on heuristics))_

Spontaneous psi experiences are explained by coincidence, confirmation bias, false memory, and post-hoc retrofitting; cultural transmission stabilises the categories.
